Longer version: I create software systems, interventions, and design principles to reduce technology-mediated harm. From stalking and online harassment to workplace surveillance and discrimination, digital technologies built to augment connection and creativity are increasingly weaponized to control, harass, and exploit the most vulnerable among us. Existing approaches to reducing these harms focus on empowering the people affected, by creating technology that improves their ability to overcome the harms they face. But whether expressed at the level of design processes or technological artifacts, empowering marginalized people through technology creates an essential tension: how to equitably balance the benefits and burdens incurred by technological intervention. When does technology help people overcome individual, community, and structural harms, and when does it instead further their marginalization? And how can technologists anticipate the answer to this question, to ensure technology truly enables human flourishing?

I work at the intersection of human-computer interaction (HCI) and computer security and privacy (S&P) to explore these questions, by demonstrating and theorizing how to center safety and responsibility in computer science and engineering. I focus on high-stakes, multi-stakeholder contexts where digital systems play a central role in people's experiences of vulnerability. Through deep collaborations with affected communities, I map the social and technical ecosystems around these harms. I then develop frameworks for how to improve technology's impact in these ecosystems, grounded in theories of social responsibility (e.g., feminist ethics, trauma-informed care, participation). I use these frameworks to build systems and interventions that improve safety and well-being for those affected, and through this practice develop principles for safe and responsible design that are transferable across our rapidly evolving landscape of social change and technological innovation. In addition to advancing scholarly conversations around computing and design, my work centers real-world impact. I am part of the team building the Clinic to End Tech Abuse (CETA), where my work has helped provide survivors of tech-enabled intimate partner violence with volunteer security and privacy support. CETA has served hundreds of survivors in the New York City area since 2018, and earned accolades from the New York City Mayor's Office to End Gender-Based Violence. With CETA, I have consulted on U.S. Senate legislation establishing legal protections for IPV survivors, resulting in the Safe Connections Act, signed into law in 2022. I have also been invited to share my work in industry, via talks at Microsoft, Meta, and Google. Most recently, I gave a keynote address at Google Trust & Safety's workshop on protecting at-risk users.

Before this, I earned a B.A. from Princeton University in Ecology and Evolutionary Biology (EEB), a field-leading department in computational approaches to understanding the biological and social worlds. I focused on the epidemiology and control of infectious disease epidemics, and my undergraduate thesis earned the department's top prize in mathematical modeling. I also earned an M.S. from Cornell Tech in Health Tech, where I added graduate-level education in machine learning and HCI to my toolkit. In previous lives I made radio, print and multimedia journalism and ran early-stage product development at a consumer health tech startup. I've also worked as a healthcare consultant in the U.S. and taught software engineering.
